    My Digital Garden

    These are some details about how I, personally, implement my digital garden.

    Structure

    Plants in my garden live in one of three spaces: 1. Seedbed 2. Private Garden 3. Public Garden This is the only hierarchical structure I have added.

    Seedbed

    The seedbed is a place where all new ideas can be captured for later review & refinement, and is the most important element of my digital garden.

    The two most crucial aspects of the seedbed are ease of entering a new note and disciplined review so notes are quickly fleshed out or discarded.

    Private Garden

    Once a seed has germinated to the point where it is a useful, standalone note, it goes to one of two places: my private garden or my public garden.

    My private digital garden is the place for notes that are useful, but contain either sensitive information I don't want to publish, or information that I believe is only relevant to a specific time or scenario for me (e.g. a planning note for a home improvement project).

    Ultimately though, my goal is to publish as much to my public garden as possible.

    Public Garden

    Publishing to my public digital garden serves a few purposes: - It adheres to my principle of transparency - It minimizes the need to traverse between my private and public gardens. This enables more easily making connections between notes and surfacing new, hopefully evergreen ideas. - It allows others to review and give feedback. Have any thoughts for me, drop me a note!

    If you are viewing this somewhere in the Web, you are viewing (a part of) my public digital garden. I hope you enjoy your time here!

    Infrastructure

    The notes/plants in my garden are virtually all Markdown (.md) files. There are a few reasons I've chosen to utilize Markdown: - Simplicity: Markdown offers powerful formatting via in-line syntax. This frees you from the bloat and complexity of word processors. Ever moved an image in Word only to have your text go flying? Not with Markdown. - Portability: You can write using Markdown in any text editor and countless apps can display formatted Markdown easily, meaning you're less easily locked into a software ecosystem. - Ease of publishing: ==I currently use GitHub Pages (see below)== but there are numerous options for publishing or easily converting Markdown, should I ever need to migrate.

    I cultivate and explore my Digital Garden almost entirely via Obsidian. Not only is it a Markdown-based note-taking app, it offers numerous features tailored to curating and exploring a digital garden. The most significant drawback is that syncing across devices is a subscription (and quite frankly, expensive) feature; the free features are so powerful though that I'm happy to pay it and support the developers.
